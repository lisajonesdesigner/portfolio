1.1 Introduction to User Flows

Topic Overview

User flows show how users progress from one step to another within a product. These flows help you understand what users try to achieve and the decisions they make along the way. Clear flows reveal delays, confusion points, and opportunities to simplify steps in a task.

You learn the purpose of user flows, the parts that form a flowchart, and the symbols used to map interactions. You finish this topic with a user flow analysis activity that guides you through a checkout process.

Case Study: Headspace

Case Study:
The Headspace Onboarding Evolution

Topic Overview

We often think of "design" as the final colors and illustrations. But for Headspace (the meditation app), their biggest design challenge wasn't how to make it look pretty—it was how to stop users from quitting before they even started.

This case study shows how they used iterative prototyping to fix a broken user flow.

The video below features a lead designer from Headspace explaining how they translate abstract concepts (like meditation) into tangible UI elements.

The Problem

Headspace noticed a major drop-off during sign-up. Their original onboarding asked users to commit to a "program" immediately. It was too much pressure, too soon. Users were overwhelmed and closing the app.

Phase 1: Low-Fidelity Exploration (The "Crazy 8s")

The design team didn't start with polished graphics. They started with sharpies and paper. They sketched dozens of different ways to ask the question: "What do you want to focus on?"

  • Why this matters: By sketching on paper, they could throw away bad ideas in seconds without wasting hours in Figma.

Phase 2: Wireframing the Logic

They selected the best sketches and built grayscale wireframes. They tested a new idea: instead of a long list of text, what if they used big, tappable "cards" for goals like Sleep, Focus, or Anxiety?

  • The Test: They showed these gray wireframes to users. Users loved the "Card" concept but were confused by the "Next" button logic. The team fixed this structure before adding any art.

Phase 3: High-Fidelity & Delight

Only once the flow was proven did they add the famous Headspace illustrations. The final result wasn't just a form; it was a conversation.

The Result

By prototyping and testing early, they reduced the "time-to-meditate" and significantly increased their sign-up conversion rate. They proved that clarity (wireframing) comes before delight (visual design).
