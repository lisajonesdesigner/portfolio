Designing a curriculum that works across three learning environments

A UX unit built to practise the same usability principles it teaches, inside an LMS that usually ignores them. User Flows and Wireframes is a blended six-hour unit teaching adult UX learners to map user flows, evaluate onboarding experiences, and translate findings into low-fidelity wireframes. It was designed, written, and built end-to-end in D2L Brightspace, across in-person, synchronous online, and self-paced delivery.

Audience Adult UX learners: early-career designers and professionals upskilling, with mixed digital experience
Tools D2L Brightspace · H5P · Miro · Figma · FeedbackFruits
Role Sole learning designer and developer
Launch unit

The complete working unit, recreated outside the university LMS. Every page, activity, and knowledge check is explorable.

The learning problem

Before designing anything, I evaluated existing university LMS spaces against structured criteria across six categories: ease of use, content quality, design and aesthetics, assessment and feedback, motivation and engagement, and outcomes. The pattern was familiar to anyone who has studied online. Content sat sometimes on the page and sometimes behind a link to a separate page, forcing a constant click-out-read-navigate-back loop that my evaluation notes described as "really tiring and confusing." Assessment information was duplicated across areas and written differently in each, so working out what to actually do meant cross-referencing. Searching "assessment" surfaced a page about Zoom. Heading text failed W3C contrast standards, and nothing worked on mobile.

The evaluation revealed a familiar pattern in the LMS environments I reviewed: link-heavy structures that fragmented attention and buried important information. Adult UX learners arriving with mixed digital experience and limited time needed something that worked with their attention, not against it.

From my evaluation notes

"The content is sometimes on the page and sometimes you have to click a link to access a separate page to read the content, then navigate back, which gets really tiring and confusing. … Because the assessment content is found in multiple places, it was difficult to work out what to do."

The learner and context

User Flows and Wireframes is a six-hour unit for adult UX learners: early-career designers and professionals upskilling into UX roles, with mixed digital experience and mixed comfort with online tools. Adult learners bring existing commitments and prior experience they expect a course to respect, not ignore. That shaped two decisions before any content was written: the unit needed more than one delivery mode, because a single format cannot serve learners with different schedules and technology comfort equally well, and it needed to teach through a project a working designer would actually be given, not an abstract exercise.

The design challenge

The challenge was twofold. Build a unit that teaches user flows and usability heuristics inside the same kind of LMS that fails those heuristics, without repeating the sins it teaches students to spot. And do it across three different delivery environments — in-person, synchronous online, and self-paced — each with different constraints on attention, interaction, and pacing, while still feeling like one coherent unit rather than three disconnected pieces stitched together.

Learning outcomes

Three objectives span Bloom's cognitive levels from Apply to Evaluate to Create. By the end of the unit, learners will be able to:

  1. Explain and apply user-flow principles to model user journeys within digital products. (Apply)
  2. Evaluate onboarding experiences using usability heuristics and competitor analysis. (Evaluate)
  3. Design and annotate low-fidelity wireframes that demonstrate clear structure, logic, and accessibility. (Create)

Curriculum architecture

The unit moves learners through three two-hour sessions, and all three converge on one authentic project: designing the onboarding flow for NSURE, a fictional insurance app, exactly as a junior designer would in practice. Each objective is pitched at a named Bloom's level and assessed by that same integrated project, following Biggs and Tang's constructive alignment: nothing is assessed that isn't taught, and nothing is taught that doesn't feed the final artefact.

The full unit was authored end-to-end in a D2L Brightspace sandpit: module and topic architecture, session pages, activity pages, and templates, all built around that single through-line project rather than around the LMS's default page structure.

Designing for three delivery modes

Each mode was matched to the cognitive work it does best, rather than distributing content evenly across modes for its own sake. In-person time goes where dialogue and error-spotting matter most, live online sessions power collaborative evaluation in breakouts, and creation happens self-paced where learners control their own tools and tempo.

Session 1 · 2 hrs · In-person

Foundations of user flows

Flow symbols, logic, and error paths. Learners map a real checkout process, then chart the NSURE onboarding journey in groups. Outcome: accurate user-flow diagrams with explained logic (LO1).

Session 2 · 2 hrs · Synchronous online

Heuristics and competitor analysis

Nielsen's heuristics applied in breakout workshops, then a structured analysis of direct and indirect competitors. Outcome: evaluated interfaces and patterns to inform wireframes (LO2).

Session 3 · 2 hrs · Self-paced

From flows to wireframes

Wireframing principles, screen planning, and annotated low-fidelity wireframes uploaded for assessment, with a mandatory reflection journal. Outcome: wireframes meeting usability and accessibility criteria (LO3).

Instructional strategy

Pedagogical grounding

The unit design is grounded in constructive alignment (Biggs and Tang), demonstrated directly in the objective-to-assessment mapping in Assessment and Feedback below, with Nielsen's usability heuristics doing double duty as both subject matter and design standard for the unit itself. Adult learning principles (Knowles) and active learning (Laurillard) shaped the delivery-mode decisions above throughout.

Assessment and feedback

The curriculum design document specifies every session down to timed activities with paired student and educator instructions. Its spine is the three objectives above, each with a direct line to the assessment criteria.

Explain and apply user-flow principles to model user journeys within digital products.ApplyAccuracy and clarity of user-flow logic in the NSURE onboarding flow.

H5P knowledge checks are embedded inside each session, giving learners instant feedback rather than waiting for a marked submission. Accessibility ran through the build rather than being retrofitted: plain language, alt text on every image, captioned video, colour contrast checked, downloadable text versions of activities, and keyboard-navigable quizzes.

Key design decisions

Content on the page, not behind links

Every topic overview, lesson list, and activity brief is readable in place, with accordions providing progressive disclosure instead of separate pages. Each click a course demands is a cost; the evaluation showed how quickly those costs compound into frustration. Reducing them is the same discipline the unit teaches for onboarding flows.

One authentic project across all three sessions

Rather than separate exercises per session, all three objectives converge on the NSURE onboarding flow. A single continuous project mirrors how a junior designer would actually encounter this work, and lets each session's output feed directly into the next.

Reflection made mandatory

The reflection journal started as optional. Peer review flagged that it would likely be skipped as a result, so it became mandatory: metacognition doesn't happen if it's the first thing learners drop under time pressure.

Evaluation

The complete unit was built and is explorable above, and was validated through structured peer review rather than delivery with a real cohort. The draft curriculum document went through structured peer review in FeedbackFruits.

What peer review found What changed
Content repeated across session mini-lectures, creating overlap.Streamlined so sessions build progressively without repetition.
The reflection journal, as an optional task, was likely to be skipped.Made the reflection journal mandatory to support metacognition.
Accessibility coverage needed to go further than the initial build.Added captioned media and clearly labelled downloads.
What the evidence shows

Complete unit built and explorable: three sessions, twenty-nine topic pages, six aligned assessment tasks, embedded knowledge checks, and a real-world case study. Structured peer review completed, with every piece of feedback actioned and documented before the final build.

What was not measured

Live delivery with a real cohort. Completion of the mandatory reflections and quiz performance against the 80% threshold. Quality of submitted wireframe artefacts against the marking criteria. Whether learners transfer flow-mapping and heuristic evaluation to their own work afterward.

Design validation

The unit was fully designed and built in a D2L sandpit. Evaluation focused on the learning architecture, usability, and alignment of the learning experience through structured peer review. Live learner outcome data was not collected, because the unit was not delivered to a live cohort.

Outcome and reflection

The evaluation-first approach earned its keep: designing against documented failure modes produced sharper decisions than designing from preference. Two things I would change. I would pilot the unit with a real cohort early, because assumptions about activity timing survive peer review but rarely survive learners. And I would design social engagement into assessed activities from the start; the evaluation showed that discussion forums go unused unless participation matters, so relying on optional forum posts, even lightly, was optimism the evidence didn't support.
